1 · What Is Algorithmic Trading?

Algorithmic trading — also called algo trading, automated trading, or systematic trading — means using a computer program to automatically buy and sell financial instruments based on a predefined set of rules.

Instead of a human sitting at a screen, watching charts, and clicking "buy" or "sell," an algorithm does it. The rules can be simple — "buy NIFTY 50 ETF when the 20-day moving average crosses above the 50-day moving average" — or extraordinarily complex, involving machine learning models that process thousands of data points per second.

The key word is predefined. Every decision the algorithm makes was programmed in advance. The algorithm does not guess. It does not feel fear or greed. It executes exactly the logic it was given, at a speed no human can match.

Simple definition: Algo trading = a set of rules + a computer that executes those rules automatically, faster and more consistently than any human ever could.

A real-world example anyone can understand

Imagine you believe that whenever RELIANCE stock drops more than 3% in a single day on high volume, it tends to bounce back within 3 days. Manually, you'd need to watch the stock every day, remember your rule, and place the trade at the right moment — without panicking.

With an algorithm, you write this rule once. The program monitors RELIANCE automatically, every minute of every trading day. The moment the condition triggers — 3% drop, high volume — it places the buy order instantly. Three days later, it sells. Every time. Without fail. That is algo trading at its most basic level.

3 · How Does an Algo Actually Work?

Every algo trading system — from the simplest retail strategy to the most complex institutional system — follows this same 7-step workflow.

The Algo Trading Workflow — Step by Step
1
Data ingestion
The algorithm receives live market data — price, volume, order book depth, news feeds, economic data. Institutional algos receive data in microseconds via co-location servers at the exchange.
2
Signal generation
The algo processes data and checks for defined conditions. "Has the RSI crossed below 30 AND is the price above the 200-day moving average?" If yes, a buy signal is generated.
3
Risk check
Before placing any order, the system checks: maximum position size, daily loss limit, existing positions, available margin. If any risk limit would be breached, the order is blocked.
4
Order generation
The algo creates a specific order: instrument (NIFTY50), order type (market/limit/SL), quantity, price, validity. It chooses the order type based on its execution strategy.
5
Order routing & execution
The order is sent through a broker's API to NSE/BSE. For retail algos: Zerodha Kite Connect, Upstox API, Angel SmartAPI. For institutional: DMA (Direct Market Access) for sub-millisecond execution.
6
Position monitoring
After the trade is live, the algo monitors the position against exit conditions: target profit, stop loss, trailing stop, or time-based exit.
7
Exit & logging
When exit conditions are met, the algo closes the position. Every trade — entry, exit, P&L, execution time — is logged. This data feeds back into strategy improvement.

4 · The 5 Main Types of Algo Trading Strategies

Strategy 1: Trend Following

What it does: Identifies when a stock is moving consistently in one direction and rides that trend until it reverses.

Indian market example: Moving average crossover on NIFTY 50 futures — go long when the 20-day MA crosses above the 50-day MA, exit when it crosses back below.

Works best in: Trending markets. Struggles in sideways, choppy conditions. Difficulty: Beginner.

Strategy 2: Mean Reversion

What it does: Bets that prices which have moved too far from their average will snap back. "What goes too high will come down."

Indian market example: When HDFC Bank's RSI drops below 25 (oversold) and the stock trades 2 standard deviations below its 20-day average — buy. Sell when RSI returns above 50.

Works best in: Range-bound markets. Dangerous in strong trending markets. Difficulty: Beginner–Intermediate.

Strategy 3: Arbitrage

What it does: Exploits price differences for the same asset across different markets or instruments simultaneously.

Indian market example: Cash-futures arbitrage on NIFTY — buy NIFTY 50 ETF and simultaneously sell NIFTY futures when the futures trade at an unusually high premium. Difficulty: Expert.

Strategy 4: Market Making

What it does: Simultaneously places buy orders below and sell orders above the current price, profiting from the bid-ask spread — repeated thousands of times daily. In India, primarily done by SEBI-registered Market Makers. Difficulty: Expert.

Strategy 5: Statistical Arbitrage

What it does: Uses statistical relationships between multiple securities to find temporary mispricings.

Indian market example: Pairs trading HDFCBANK and ICICIBANK — when the price ratio diverges beyond 2 standard deviations, go long the underperformer and short the outperformer. Difficulty: Advanced.

5 · Who Uses Algo Trading in India?

Group 1: Institutional players

Mutual funds, insurance companies, FIIs, and hedge funds. Their goal is executing massive orders — sometimes ₹500 crore or more — without moving the market price against themselves. They use VWAP and TWAP execution algorithms to spread orders across the trading day.

Group 2: Proprietary trading firms

Firms that trade only their own capital using sophisticated algorithms. Companies like Optiver, Jane Street, and Indian prop desks run high-frequency strategies with co-location at NSE's data center. These firms require crores in infrastructure investment.

Group 3: Retail algo traders (that's you)

Individual traders using broker APIs and platforms like Zerodha Streak, Tradetron, or Python scripts to automate their own strategies. Capital can start at ₹25,000. No co-location needed. This series is built specifically for this group.

6 · Is Algo Trading Legal in India? SEBI Rules Explained

Yes — algo trading is completely legal in India. SEBI first permitted it in April 2008. But there are important rules every retail trader must know.

YearSEBI actionImpact
2008First DMA and algo trading circularInstitutional algo trading officially permitted

2013Algo audit requirementsBrokers must audit all algos before deployment

2024Regulations tightenedStricter compliance for third-party algo platforms

Rule 1: Every algo must be broker-approved. Your algorithm must go through a SEBI-registered broker. You cannot connect directly to NSE.

Rule 2: Only use regulated third-party platforms. Platforms like Tradetron and Streak must operate within the broker's regulatory framework.

Rule 3: All SEBI limits apply. Position limits, margin requirements, and order-to-trade ratio (OTR) limits apply to algo orders exactly like manual orders.

Rule 4: No guaranteed returns — ever. Any service guaranteeing returns from an algo is illegal under SEBI rules.

Bottom line: Use an algo through Zerodha Kite Connect, Upstox API, or Angel One SmartAPI — you are operating legally. Use an unregistered platform promising guaranteed returns — you are taking legal and financial risk.

9 · 8 Beginner Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Skipping backtesting

"It makes logical sense" is not enough. Markets are not logical. Test your strategy on data first before risking real capital. Full backtesting guide in Blog #4.

Mistake 2: Over-optimizing (curve fitting)

Tweaking your strategy until it looks perfect on historical data — then failing in live trading. A 95% win rate on 2020–2024 data tuned specifically to that period will fail in 2025. Solution: out-of-sample testing and walk-forward analysis.

Mistake 3: No risk management

Running an algo without hard stop-loss and daily loss limits is like driving at 200 km/h with no brakes. Every single algo must have a per-trade stop loss, a maximum daily loss limit, and a maximum drawdown limit.

Mistake 4: Not paper trading first

Always run your algo with fake money in live market conditions before risking real capital. Reveals API delays, logic errors, and slippage you'd never see in backtesting.

Mistake 5: Too much capital too soon

Start with the minimum capital needed to trade one unit. Scale up only after 3–6 months of live results matching your backtest.

Mistake 6: Ignoring transaction costs

Brokerage, STT, exchange fees, GST, SEBI charges — these add up to 0.05–0.1% per trade. An algo trading 10 times daily faces 0.5–1% in daily costs. Always include realistic costs in your backtests.

Mistake 7: Not monitoring your live algo

APIs disconnect. Strategies degrade as market conditions change. Check performance weekly and have a manual kill switch ready for emergencies.

Mistake 8: Using guaranteed return services

These are scams or dangerously over-leveraged strategies. SEBI has taken action against many such services. No legitimate algo service guarantees returns.

10 · Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to know coding to do algo trading in India?
No. Platforms like Zerodha Streak and Tradetron allow no-code strategy building using drag-and-drop. Learning Python gives you significantly more power, but is not required to start. Blogs #3 and #5 in this series cover both paths.
How much money do I need to start algo trading in India?
You can learn and backtest with zero capital using free platforms. For live trading, ₹25,000–₹50,000 is a reasonable starting amount. Start small and scale only after proving your strategy works in live markets over at least 3 months.
Can algo trading make me rich?
It can be very profitable for systematic traders who backtest rigorously and manage risk carefully. It is not passive income or a get-rich-quick path. The learning curve takes 6–18 months before most traders find a consistently profitable strategy.
What is the difference between algo trading and HFT?
All HFT is algo trading, but not all algo trading is HFT. HFT executes thousands of trades per second using co-located servers for microsecond advantages. Retail traders can do regular algo trading — HFT requires institutional infrastructure worth crores.
Which broker is best for algo trading beginners in India?
Zerodha (most popular, best documentation, Streak platform included), Upstox (free API, growing community), and Angel One (free SmartAPI). All three are SEBI-registered and reliable. Blog #5 covers all platforms with a full comparison table.

Order Types, Execution & Market Mechanics

1 · Types of Orders Every Algo Trader Must Know

Before your algorithm can place a single trade, it needs to know what kind of order to send. Every order type has different behaviour, cost, and risk profile — choosing the wrong one can cost you significantly in slippage or missed fills.

Market Order

What it is: An order to buy or sell immediately at the best available price. Your order gets filled instantly — but you have no control over the price you get.

When to use: When speed matters more than price. Entry on a fast-moving momentum signal. Exiting a position when your stop loss triggers.

Risk: In illiquid instruments (small-cap stocks, low-volume options, thin crypto pairs), market orders can have severe slippage — you might intend to buy at ₹100 but actually buy at ₹100.80.

Limit Order

What it is: An order that only executes at your specified price or better. A buy limit order at ₹100 will only fill at ₹100 or lower.

When to use: When price control matters more than immediacy. Mean reversion entries (waiting for price to come to you). Building positions in liquid instruments.

Risk: Your order may not fill at all if the price never reaches your limit. In fast markets, this means missed opportunities.

Stop-Loss Order (SL)

What it is: An order that triggers a market or limit order when the price reaches a specified level. The trigger price is your stop; once hit, it sends the underlying order to the exchange.

SL-Market: When your stop triggers, a market order is sent. Fast execution but slippage risk in volatile conditions.

SL-Limit: When your stop triggers, a limit order is sent. Price controlled but may not fill in fast moves.

Bracket Order (BO)

What it is: A single order that automatically includes both a target (take profit) and a stop-loss. Zerodha, Upstox, and most Indian brokers support bracket orders. Extremely useful for algo trading because your risk and reward are defined at entry.

Cover Order (CO)

What it is: An order with a compulsory stop-loss. Allows higher leverage than normal because the broker knows your maximum loss is defined.

Algo recommendation: For most beginners, use SL-Market orders for entries and exits. Price control is less important than consistent execution. As you advance, move to limit orders for better fills on mean reversion strategies.

2 · How the Order Book Works

Every exchange — NSE, BSE, or Delta Exchange — operates an order book. This is simply a list of all pending buy and sell orders at every price level.

The bid price is the highest price a buyer is willing to pay. The ask price is the lowest price a seller is willing to accept. The difference between them is the bid-ask spread — this is a real cost you pay on every trade.

When your algo places a market buy order, it immediately matches against the best ask price in the order book. When it places a limit buy order, it sits in the order book at your specified price waiting for a seller to match.

3 · What Is Slippage and Why It Destroys Backtests

Slippage is the difference between the price you expected to execute at and the price you actually got. It is one of the most underestimated costs in algo trading and the reason many strategies that look profitable in backtesting fail in live trading.

Sources of slippage: Market impact (your large order moves the price), speed (market moved between signal and order), bid-ask spread (you pay the spread on every trade), and partial fills (your order only partially filled at your target price).

How to account for it: Add at least 0.05% slippage per trade to every backtest on liquid NSE instruments. For options or illiquid stocks, add 0.1–0.2%. For crypto on Delta Exchange, add 0.02–0.05% for liquid BTC pairs.

4 · How Broker APIs Work

A broker API is the bridge between your algorithm running on a computer and the exchange. Without it, your algo has no way to place real trades. Here is how it works step by step:

  1. Your algorithm generates a signal (e.g. "buy NIFTY 50 futures")
  2. It sends an HTTP request or WebSocket message to your broker's API server (e.g. Zerodha Kite Connect)
  3. The broker's server validates the request (checks margin, daily limits, SEBI rules)
  4. The broker routes your order to NSE's matching engine
  5. NSE matches your order against the order book
  6. The execution confirmation is sent back through the broker API to your algorithm
  7. Your algo updates its position tracking and logs the trade

The entire process — from your signal to confirmed execution — takes 50–200 milliseconds for retail algo traders using standard broker APIs. Institutional algos using DMA (Direct Market Access) and co-location at NSE's data center can execute in under 1 millisecond.

For Delta Exchange crypto: The process is identical. Delta Exchange provides a REST API for order placement and a WebSocket API for real-time market data. Python SDK available with full documentation at docs.delta.exchange.

What is the difference between NSE and BSE for algo trading?
For retail algo traders, NSE is the primary choice. NSE has significantly higher liquidity, tighter bid-ask spreads, faster execution, and better API support. BSE is used for some equity delivery trades but NSE dominates derivatives and is the standard for algo trading.

Your First Strategy: Moving Averages, RSI & Rule-Based Systems

1 · What Makes a Good Algo Strategy?

A good algo strategy has three properties: it has a clear, testable logic, it works across different market conditions (not just one specific period), and the risk/reward makes sense after accounting for transaction costs.

Before you code anything, you must be able to write your strategy in plain if/then language. If you cannot do this, you are not ready to code it. This guide will build two complete strategies — one for NSE and one for crypto — that you can start testing today.

2 · Strategy #1: Moving Average Crossover (MA Cross)

This is the most popular beginner algo strategy in the world. It works on any liquid market — NIFTY futures, Bank NIFTY, BTC perpetuals on Delta Exchange.

The logic: When a short-term moving average crosses above a long-term moving average, the market is entering an uptrend — buy. When the short-term crosses below the long-term, exit or go short.

The rules (plain language):

  • Calculate the 20-day simple moving average (SMA20)
  • Calculate the 50-day simple moving average (SMA50)
  • When SMA20 crosses above SMA50 — enter long (buy)
  • When SMA20 crosses below SMA50 — exit long (sell)
  • Stop loss: 2% below entry price
  • Position size: 2% of total capital per trade

Applying it to NIFTY: Use NIFTY 50 futures or NIFTY ETF (like Nippon India NIFTY BeES). Run on daily closing prices. Backtest on NSE historical data from 2015–2024.

Applying it to crypto: Use BTC/USDT perpetual on Delta Exchange. Run on 4-hour candles for more signals. Same crossover logic applies exactly.

Historical context: The 20/50 MA crossover on NIFTY 50 has historically captured the major bull runs (2016–2018, 2020–2021) while limiting drawdown during crashes. It generates approximately 8–15 trades per year on daily data — low frequency, low friction.

3 · Strategy #2: RSI Mean Reversion

RSI (Relative Strength Index) measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes to identify overbought and oversold conditions. It ranges from 0–100. Below 30 = oversold (potential buy). Above 70 = overbought (potential sell).

The rules (plain language):

  • Calculate 14-period RSI on daily closing prices
  • When RSI drops below 30 AND price is above the 200-day MA (confirming the overall trend is up) — enter long
  • When RSI rises above 60 — exit long
  • Stop loss: 3% below entry price
  • Position size: 1.5% of total capital per trade
  • Maximum 3 simultaneous open positions

Why the 200-day MA filter? RSI below 30 in a downtrending market can keep going much lower. The 200-day MA filter ensures you only buy dips in fundamentally uptrending instruments — significantly improving the win rate.

On NSE: Works well on large-cap stocks (Reliance, HDFC Bank, Infosys) and NIFTY indices. These are mean-reverting by nature.

On crypto: Works on ETH/USDT and BNB/USDT on Delta Exchange. BTC is slightly more trending, making mean reversion less reliable for BTC specifically.

4 · How to Turn Rules into Code (Python)

Here is the exact Python code for the MA Crossover strategy using the pandas library and Zerodha historical data:

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

# Load your historical data
df = pd.read_csv('nifty_daily.csv')

# Calculate moving averages
df['SMA20'] = df['close'].rolling(20).mean()
df['SMA50'] = df['close'].rolling(50).mean()

# Generate signals
df['signal'] = ''
df.loc[(df['SMA20'] > df['SMA50']) & (df['SMA20'].shift(1) <= df['SMA50'].shift(1)), 'signal'] = 'BUY'
df.loc[(df['SMA20'] < df['SMA50']) & (df['SMA20'].shift(1) >= df['SMA50'].shift(1)), 'signal'] = 'SELL'

This code works identically for NIFTY data from NSE and for BTC/USDT data from Delta Exchange — just swap the CSV file. The signal logic is universal.

5 · Testing Your Strategy Before Using Real Money

Never deploy a strategy without seeing its historical performance. The next guide (Guide #4) covers backtesting in full detail, but here is the minimum you must check before moving forward:

  • Win rate: What percentage of trades were profitable? A 40% win rate can still be profitable if wins are 3x bigger than losses.
  • Profit factor: Total profit divided by total loss. Must be above 1.5 for a viable strategy.
  • Maximum drawdown: The worst peak-to-trough loss in your backtest. You must be emotionally and financially able to survive this drawdown in live trading.
  • Number of trades: A strategy with only 5 trades in a backtest is statistically meaningless. You need at least 30–50 trades for any meaningful conclusion.

Backtesting 101: Test Your Strategy on 10 Years of Data

1 · Why Backtesting Is Non-Negotiable

Backtesting is running your strategy rules against historical market data to see how it would have performed. It is the closest thing to a time machine in trading — you can test 10 years of market conditions in seconds before risking a single rupee.

Without backtesting, you are deploying an untested strategy on live capital. This is how most retail algo traders lose money in their first year. The strategy "made sense" to them logically, but logic alone is not evidence — market data is.

The most important caveat: Backtesting shows you what would have happened. It does not guarantee what will happen. The goal is to find strategies with positive expectancy across many different market conditions — not to find the "perfect" strategy that only worked in one specific period.

2 · The Curve-Fitting Trap (The Biggest Backtesting Mistake)

Curve fitting (also called over-optimization) is when you tweak your strategy parameters until they look perfect on historical data. A strategy optimized this way will have near-perfect backtest results — and will almost certainly fail in live trading.

Example: You test RSI with thresholds of 30/70, 25/75, 20/80, and 35/65. You find that 22/68 gives the best backtest results. You use 22/68. This is curve fitting — you have tuned the strategy to fit the noise of that specific historical period, not to capture a real market edge.

The solution: Out-of-sample testing. Split your data into two parts: training data (70%) and test data (30%). Optimize on training data only. Then run your optimized strategy on the test data — which it has never seen. If it performs well on both, you have a genuine edge. If it only works on training data, you have curve fitting.

3 · Walk-Forward Analysis

Walk-forward analysis is the gold standard of backtesting. Instead of optimizing once, you roll through time: optimize on a 2-year window, test on the next 6 months, roll forward, optimize again on the next 2 years, test on the next 6 months, and so on.

This simulates how you would actually use the strategy in live trading — periodically re-optimizing as market conditions change, and measuring true out-of-sample performance at each step.

Risk Management: Position Sizing, Stop Losses & Drawdown Control

1 · Why Risk Management Is More Important Than Strategy

A mediocre strategy with excellent risk management will outperform a great strategy with poor risk management over time. This is not a philosophical statement — it is mathematically provable. Risk management determines your survival as a trader. Strategy determines your edge once you have survived.

The hard truth: Most algo traders who blow up their accounts do not have bad strategies. They have good strategies with no risk management. A strategy that wins 60% of the time and has a 20% drawdown in backtesting will have a 40–50% drawdown in live trading if not managed properly.

2 · Position Sizing — The Kelly Criterion & Fixed Fractional

Fixed Fractional Position Sizing is the most practical method for retail algo traders. Risk a fixed percentage of your total capital on each trade — typically 1–2%.

Formula: Position Size = (Account Size × Risk %) ÷ (Entry Price − Stop Loss Price)

Example: Account = ₹5,00,000. Risk per trade = 1% = ₹5,000. Entry = ₹2,800. Stop loss = ₹2,730. Risk per share = ₹70. Position size = ₹5,000 ÷ ₹70 = 71 shares.

This formula works identically for NSE stocks, NIFTY futures, and Delta Exchange crypto positions. The currency changes (₹ vs USDT) but the math is identical.

4 · Daily Loss Limit and Circuit Breakers

Every professional algo trading system has a daily loss limit — a hard stop that halts all trading if total losses for the day exceed a threshold. This prevents a single bad day from wiping out weeks or months of gains.

Recommended daily loss limits:

  • Conservative: Stop trading if daily loss exceeds 1% of total capital
  • Moderate: Stop trading if daily loss exceeds 2% of total capital
  • Aggressive: Stop trading if daily loss exceeds 3% of total capital

On NSE: implement this in your Python code by checking cumulative P&L before each new order. On Delta Exchange: Delta Exchange has built-in daily P&L tracking via the API — check account.total_pnl before placing orders.

5 · Maximum Drawdown — Know Your Number Before You Start

Maximum drawdown (MDD) is the largest peak-to-trough loss in your backtest. Before you deploy any strategy, you must look at this number and honestly ask yourself: "Can I emotionally and financially survive this drawdown in live trading?"

If your backtest shows a 25% maximum drawdown and you are deploying ₹2,00,000, that means you must be prepared to see your account drop to ₹1,50,000 before potentially recovering. If that would cause you to shut down the strategy in a panic — you should reduce your position sizes until the expected drawdown is survivable.

1 · Why Paper Trading Is Not Optional

Paper trading means running your algo in a live market environment with simulated money. It costs nothing and catches problems that backtesting never reveals: API connection issues, order rejection errors, timing bugs, logic errors that only appear with live data, and execution quality.

Every professional algo trader paper trades a new strategy for a minimum of 2–4 weeks before going live. Skip this step and you are essentially using real money to paper trade — an expensive way to debug.

Run in paper trade mode for at least 20 trading days (approximately one month). During this time, monitor: how many signals are generated (does it match your backtest frequency?), execution timing, and whether the paper P&L roughly matches what your backtest predicted.

Advanced Strategies: Pairs Trading, Momentum & Statistical Arbitrage

1 · Pairs Trading on NSE

Pairs trading identifies two instruments that historically move together — their prices are correlated. When they temporarily diverge beyond a statistical threshold, you go long the underperformer and short the outperformer, betting they will converge back to their historical relationship.

Classic Indian market pairs: HDFC Bank vs ICICI Bank (both private sector banks, highly correlated). TCS vs Infosys (both IT large caps). Reliance vs IOC (both energy sector). NIFTY vs BANKNIFTY (correlated indices).

Implementation steps:

  1. Calculate the price ratio: Ratio = Price_A / Price_B
  2. Calculate the mean and standard deviation of this ratio over a rolling 60-day window
  3. Calculate the Z-score: Z = (Current Ratio − Mean) / Std Dev
  4. When Z > +2.0: Ratio is too high → Short Stock A, Long Stock B
  5. When Z < −2.0: Ratio is too low → Long Stock A, Short Stock B
  6. Exit when Z returns to 0 (mean reversion complete)

2 · Pairs Trading on Crypto

The same strategy applies to crypto on Delta Exchange. BTC vs ETH is the most traded crypto pair — both are highly liquid on Delta Exchange with full perpetual contracts available.

Historical correlation between BTC and ETH is above 0.85 — very high. When ETH diverges from its typical BTC relationship, pairs trading generates opportunities. The advantage over NSE pairs: crypto markets are open 24/7 and have more frequent divergence events.

3 · Momentum Strategies

Momentum strategies buy instruments that have recently performed well and short (or avoid) instruments that have recently performed poorly. "The trend is your friend" taken to a systematic extreme.

Cross-sectional momentum on NSE (NIFTY 50): Rank all 50 stocks in NIFTY by their 12-month return (minus the most recent month). Buy the top 10 stocks. Hold for one month. Rebalance. This is the classic momentum factor and it has historically delivered excess returns in Indian markets.

Time-series momentum on crypto: If BTC's 90-day return is positive, hold BTC long. If negative, hold cash (USDT) or go short. Simple rule, proven in research, applicable directly on Delta Exchange perpetuals.

4 · Statistical Arbitrage

Stat arb generalizes pairs trading to baskets of instruments. Instead of two stocks, you build a statistically constructed "spread" from multiple instruments that should theoretically mean-revert. The math (cointegration, Johansen test) is more complex but the edge is larger and more robust.

For advanced practitioners: Look into cointegration testing using the Engle-Granger two-step method. Python's statsmodels library has full cointegration testing built in. Start with pairs before moving to baskets.

How NSE and BSE Actually Work

1 · The Structure of Indian Capital Markets

India's capital markets are regulated by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) and operate through two primary exchanges: the National Stock Exchange (NSE) and the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE). Understanding how these exchanges work at a structural level is the foundation of every trading and investing decision you will ever make.

NSE — The National Stock Exchange

Founded in 1992 and starting operations in 1994, NSE is India's largest stock exchange by trading volume. NSE processes over ₹12 lakh crore in total daily turnover when you combine cash equities, equity derivatives (futures and options), currency derivatives, and debt markets. NSE operates the NIFTY family of indices and is the primary exchange for derivatives trading in India.

NSE's matching engine — the system that matches buy and sell orders — uses a price-time priority system: orders at better prices are executed first, and among orders at the same price, earlier orders are executed first. This is standard across all major global exchanges.

BSE — The Bombay Stock Exchange

Founded in 1875, BSE is Asia's oldest stock exchange. While BSE has fewer derivatives transactions than NSE, it has more listed companies (over 5,000 vs NSE's approximately 2,000). BSE operates the SENSEX index — India's most famous market benchmark, tracking the 30 largest and most liquid BSE-listed companies.

For retail investors and traders, NSE is the primary trading venue — it has tighter bid-ask spreads, higher liquidity, and better API support for algo trading. BSE is relevant for equity delivery trades, especially for stocks only listed on BSE, and for the SME platform.

2 · How Your Trade Actually Gets Executed

The Journey of a Trade — From Click to Settlement
1
You place an order in your broker app
You open Zerodha, Upstox, or Angel One and click buy/sell. Your broker's front-end application sends your order to the broker's server via their app or API.
2
Broker validates your order
The broker checks: do you have sufficient margin or balance? Is the order within SEBI position limits? Is the instrument tradeable right now? If all checks pass, the order is routed to the exchange.
3
Order reaches NSE/BSE matching engine
Your order enters the exchange's electronic order book. If a matching counter-order exists, your trade executes immediately. If not, your order waits in the book until a match arrives.
4
Trade confirmation sent back
NSE sends a trade confirmation back through your broker to you. This typically happens in 50–200 milliseconds for standard retail orders.
5
Clearing and settlement — T+1
India moved to T+1 settlement in 2023 — the fastest in the world. This means if you buy shares today (T), the shares are credited to your demat account and your money is debited by tomorrow (T+1). Previously T+2.
6
Shares held in your demat account
Your shares are held electronically in a demat account maintained by NSDL (National Securities Depository Limited) or CDSL (Central Depository Services Limited). These are the two depositories in India.

4 · What SEBI Does and Why It Matters

SEBI is the market regulator — equivalent to the SEC in the USA or FCA in the UK. SEBI's primary responsibilities include regulating stock exchanges, brokers, and investment advisers; protecting investor interests; promoting market development; and preventing fraudulent and unfair trade practices.

Key SEBI rules that directly affect you as a retail trader: margin requirements (how much capital you need to hold a position), position limits (maximum exposure per instrument), broker regulations (who can operate as a broker and under what rules), and the algo trading framework (what retail traders can and cannot do algorithmically).

NIFTY 50 and Bank NIFTY: Everything You Need to Know

1 · What Is NIFTY 50?

NIFTY 50 is India's flagship stock market index, maintained by NSE Indices Limited (a subsidiary of NSE). It tracks the performance of the 50 largest and most liquid companies listed on NSE, across 13 sectors of the Indian economy.

NIFTY 50 is a free-float market capitalization weighted index. This means each company's influence on the index is proportional to the market value of shares available for trading (free float), not the total company value. Reliance Industries, HDFC Bank, and Infosys typically have the highest weights.

NIFTY 50 is reviewed semi-annually (March and September). Companies that no longer meet the inclusion criteria are replaced. The index was launched on November 3, 1995, with a base value of 1,000.

2 · What Moves NIFTY 50?

NIFTY 50 is moved by a combination of domestic and global factors. Understanding these drivers helps you anticipate market direction and design better algo strategies.

3 · Bank NIFTY — The High-Volatility Index

Bank NIFTY (officially NIFTY Bank) tracks the 12 most liquid banking stocks on NSE. It includes public sector banks (SBI, Bank of Baroda) and private sector banks (HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank).

Bank NIFTY is the most actively traded index derivative in the world by number of contracts. This extreme liquidity makes it the favourite instrument for intraday traders, options sellers, and algo traders in India.

Key characteristics: higher volatility than NIFTY 50 (typically 1.5–2x the daily move), extremely sensitive to RBI policy and banking sector news, weekly options expiry on Wednesdays, and very tight bid-ask spreads due to extraordinary liquidity.

4 · Trading NIFTY and Bank NIFTY with Algos

Both NIFTY 50 and Bank NIFTY have futures and options contracts on NSE. These are the most liquid derivative contracts in India and the primary instruments for algo traders. Lot sizes: NIFTY 50 = 25 units per lot, Bank NIFTY = 15 units per lot.

Delta Exchange India: Complete Beginner's Guide to Crypto Derivatives

1 · What Is Delta Exchange?

Delta Exchange is India's leading crypto derivatives exchange — a platform specifically designed for trading crypto futures and options, not spot crypto. Founded in 2018 and headquartered in Singapore, Delta Exchange is popular among Indian traders because it offers crypto derivatives trading with no restrictions for Indian users, low fees, full API access for algo trading, and deep liquidity in BTC and ETH contracts.

Spot crypto trading in India (buying actual BTC or ETH) is regulated and taxed under Indian law. Delta Exchange operates in a different regulatory framework — it deals in crypto derivatives (contracts based on crypto prices), not direct crypto ownership. This is an important distinction that makes Delta Exchange accessible and functional for Indian traders.

2 · What Are Crypto Derivatives?

A crypto derivative is a financial contract whose value is based on the price of an underlying cryptocurrency. You are not buying or selling actual Bitcoin — you are buying or selling a contract that tracks Bitcoin's price. This allows you to profit from both rising and falling prices, use leverage, and hedge your crypto holdings.

Delta Exchange offers two main types of derivatives: Perpetual Futures (no expiry date, trade continuously 24/7) and Options (contracts that give you the right but not obligation to buy or sell at a specific price).

3 · How Perpetual Futures Work on Delta Exchange

A Bitcoin perpetual futures contract (symbol: BTCUSDT or BTC-PERP) lets you speculate on Bitcoin's price without an expiry date. If you believe BTC will go up, you go long (buy). If you believe it will go down, you go short (sell). You profit or lose based on price movement, magnified by your chosen leverage.

Funding Rate: Because perpetual futures have no expiry, they use a funding rate mechanism to keep the futures price close to the spot price. Every 8 hours, long position holders pay short position holders (when funding is positive) or vice versa. Funding rate is typically 0.01–0.05% per 8 hours.

4 · Getting Started on Delta Exchange

Delta Exchange Setup — Step by Step
1
Create an account
Go to delta.exchange → Sign up with email → Complete KYC verification (passport or Aadhaar accepted).
2
Deposit USDT
Delta Exchange uses USDT (Tether) as collateral. Deposit USDT from your crypto wallet or buy USDT on an Indian exchange (CoinDCX, WazirX) first. Minimum deposit: $10.
3
Try the testnet first
Before risking real money, use testnet.delta.exchange — free USDT to practice with on real market data. Spend at least 1–2 weeks here.
4
Start with low leverage
Begin with 2x–3x leverage maximum. Higher leverage means faster liquidation. At 10x leverage, a 10% move against you liquidates your position completely.
5
For algo trading: get your API key
Settings → API Keys → Create API Key. Install delta-rest-client Python SDK. Full documentation at docs.delta.exchange.

5 · Understanding Liquidation

Liquidation happens when your position's losses consume your entire margin. At that point, Delta Exchange automatically closes your position to prevent your balance going negative. The liquidation price depends on your entry price, leverage, and position size. Higher leverage = closer liquidation price to your entry.

Most common beginner mistake: Using too much leverage. Start with 2x–5x maximum. The potential for large gains at 20x leverage is real — but so is the certainty of liquidation on any normal volatility swing. Manage leverage like a professional risk manager, not a gambler.

How AI Is Already Trading on NSE and Crypto: What Every Investor Must Know

1 · The Invisible Competitor

Most Indian retail investors believe they are competing against other humans when they trade — analysts, fund managers, experienced traders. The reality in 2025 is very different. Over 50% of the order flow on NSE is generated by algorithms. On high-volume trading days in Nifty and Bank Nifty options, this number is higher. In crypto markets on Delta Exchange, algorithmic trading accounts for an even larger percentage.

These are not simple "if price goes up, buy" rules. The most sophisticated algos use machine learning models trained on decades of market data, natural language processing models that read RBI press releases and BSE corporate filings in milliseconds, and reinforcement learning agents that continuously adapt to changing market conditions.

2 · What Institutional AI Algos Are Actually Doing

Execution algorithms: The simplest AI application. Large funds (mutual funds, FIIs) need to buy or sell hundreds of crores of stock without moving the price. ML models determine the optimal way to split and time large orders — VWAP, TWAP, and more sophisticated adaptive algorithms that respond to real-time order book conditions.

Signal generation algorithms: ML models that process thousands of data points (price, volume, earnings data, satellite imagery of factory car parks, credit card transaction data, social media sentiment) to generate buy and sell signals before human analysts can process the same information.

News trading algorithms: NLP models that read RBI monetary policy statements, SEBI circulars, quarterly earnings releases, and news articles in milliseconds and trade before human traders finish reading the headline.

Market making algorithms: AI-powered systems that continuously quote bid and ask prices in the order book, profiting from the spread while managing inventory risk using ML-based hedging models.

3 · Free AI Tools Any Retail Investor Can Use Today

Screener.in: India's best free stock screener. Not AI-powered per se, but allows you to write custom queries that filter NSE/BSE stocks on any financial metric — similar to running your own systematic screen.

ChatGPT and Claude: Ask ChatGPT to explain an RBI policy decision, summarise a company's quarterly results, or help you write Python code for your first algo strategy. The capabilities here are genuinely powerful for retail investors.

TradingView: Excellent AI-powered charting platform with strategy backtesting. The Pine Script language lets you write and test strategies on NSE data for free.

Fear & Greed Index (for crypto): A free sentiment indicator for crypto markets. Extreme Fear historically correlates with buying opportunities; Extreme Greed with selling opportunities. Available free at alternative.me.

4 · What AI Cannot Do (The Important Limits)

AI cannot predict the future. Algorithms process historical patterns and current data to generate probabilistic forecasts — but markets are non-stationary (the relationships that worked last year may not work this year), and truly novel events (COVID, demonetization, war) are impossible to predict from historical data alone.

AI also cannot replace your judgment about when market conditions have changed so dramatically that your historical models are no longer valid. The best algo traders combine systematic AI/ML models with human oversight and regime detection.
